## Bassline character

The bass defines the groove as much as the drums. Decide its *type* at idea stage:

- **Root-cycle / walking** — one note per beat outlining changes; swing and bebop default.
- **Pedal** — one held/repeated note under moving harmony; tension and modal stasis.
- **Riff / ostinato** — a repeating rhythmic figure; funk, fusion, lofi hooks.
- **Sparse / syncopated** — space and off-beat hits; laid-back neo-soul and lofi. Landing on beat 1 then leaving space reads as "relaxed."

## Dramatic arc — the backbone

A chord progression that repeats with no emotional shape is a loop, not a piece. The **dramatic arc** is the emotional journey the piece takes — from one feeling to another — and it is the master decision every other choice serves.

State the arc as a sequence of feelings mapped to sections, e.g. "doubt → hope → peak → acceptance" or "calm → longing → warmth → rest." Each feeling *requires* specific musical conditions:

- **a searching/unsure phase** wants low-mid register, melodies that stop on unresolved tones (2nd, 6th, 7th), sparse instrumentation, laid-back timing;
- **a building/hopeful phase** wants tightening rhythm, bass stepping upward, register creeping higher, instruments entering one at a time, motif repeated a step higher (sequence);
- **a peak phase** wants high register, full ensemble, the melody reaching and *holding* its highest note, open/major harmony, on-the-beat placement (confident);
- **a resolution phase** wants falling register, loosening rhythm, full resolution to tonic, space and silence, widening ambience.

The arc tells you how quiet the verse is, how the pre-chorus builds, whether the chorus opens or bursts, and how the ending lands. Design it before chords. When the person gives only a vibe, propose an arc and confirm it.

## Phrasing and tension-release

**Phrasing** is *how the melody is played*, not just which notes — it's where feeling lives. Decide per section:

- **long vs. short notes** — long notes = weight, stillness; short = urgency, playfulness;
- **gaps between phrases** — long gaps = hesitation, space to breathe; short gaps = momentum;
- **rising vs. falling contour** — rising = hope/lift; falling = settling/release;
- **the peak note** — the single highest, most-exposed note; place it at the emotional climax and consider holding it;
- **motif repetition** — repeating a figure (same, or transposed up as a sequence) builds recognition and momentum;
- **notes held before resolution** — landing on an unresolved tone and holding creates yearning;
- **on the beat vs. slightly behind** — on-beat = confident, tight; behind = relaxed, laid-back, aching.

**Tension and release** keep a piece from getting boring. Build tension, then release it — but choose only the techniques that serve the arc at that moment; stacking all of them is over-arrangement. The palette:

- a **dominant chord held** before resolving to tonic;
- a **melody stopping on an unresolved tone** (leaving it hanging);
- **bass moving contrary** to the melody (opposite directions = friction);
- **rhythm tightening** (subdivisions getting denser);
- **instruments added** one by one;
- **register climbing**;
- **volume/intensity rising**;
- **reverb/ambience widening**;
- **a beat of silence** right before a big section (makes the entrance hit);
- **broken chords** opening into space;
- **deliberate silence/space** as its own release.

The rule of thumb: a section that only builds and never releases exhausts; one that only releases and never builds drifts. The arc says which each section needs.

## Melody, motif, and hook

- **Motif** = a short, memorable rhythmic+intervallic cell. Define it by its rhythm, its interval shape (contour), and its target notes — *before* filling bars.
- **Hook** = the phrase a listener remembers in the first few seconds. In instrumentals it's carried by the lead's rhythm and contour, not lyrics. Give it a fragment form, a complete form, and a transformed return.
- **Development** > repetition. Transform the motif (transpose, invert, augment/diminish rhythm, reharmonize under it) and assign each transformation a role (statement, build, climax, resolution). Preserve space — don't turn every repeat into a new tune.
- **Guide tones** (3rds and 7ths of each chord) are the melody's landing points; targeting them makes a line "sing" the changes without spelling every note.

## Song structure

- Jazz forms: **AABA (32-bar)**, **blues (12-bar)**, **ABAC**, or **through-composed**. Loop-based forms: **intro → loop A → variation → contrast (B) → transformed return → outro**.
- Every repeated section should *vary* — reorchestrate, reharmonize, fragment, thin out, or transform it. Literal repetition with no purpose is the #1 boredom failure.
- Plan a **macro arc**: where's the climax, where's the subtraction/breakdown, where's the ending? Even a chill lofi loop benefits from one build and one drop.
- Give the piece a **finite ending** — a tag, a ritard, a final chord, or a clean loop point. Don't leave it endlessly looping with no exit decision.

## Arrangement and instrument interaction

- Assign each instrument a **role** and a **register lane** so they don't collide: bass (low, foundation), keys (mid, harmony/comping), lead (upper-mid, melody), pad (wide, glue), drums (groove).
- **Call and response** between lead and comp creates dialogue and keeps space alive.
- **Subtraction** (dropping an instrument for a section) is one of the strongest arrangement moves — it makes the re-entry hit.
- Density should track the arc: sparse intro, fuller middle, thinned ending. Decide these entrances/exits at idea stage so the plan carries them.
