# Classic jazz (Dixieland & swing big band) — genre profile

Adapted from a classic-jazz style brief (submitted 2026-07-20, translated and
restructured into this package's conventions). This deepens the "Classic
jazz" entry in `style-cheatsheets.md` the same way `neo-soul-genre.md`
deepens "Jazzhop / neo-soul" — the cheatsheet stays the fast starting point,
this file is where the sub-genre, instrumentation, and arrangement detail
lives. Other genres keep using `style-cheatsheets.md` as their starting
point; this level of depth is added per-genre only when it earns its place.

Classic jazz is the era when jazz was dancehall music: energetic,
theatrical, and grand. **Do not copy** a specific recording, artist, or
arrangement literally — translate the reference into musical attributes
(tempo, texture, harmonic density, articulation, dynamics, emotional arc).
Originality comes from the **combination** of motif + voicing + groove +
phrasing + palette + arrangement, not from exotic chord names — this era's
harmony is deliberately consonant and warm, not dense with modern
dissonance.

## Musical DNA baku

| Parameter | Default profile value |
|---|---|
| Tempo zone | 60-230+ BPM depending on sub-style (see §2 tempo matrix) — wide range because this genre spans slow blues to burning Dixieland |
| Meter | 4/4 (occasionally 2/4-felt over a 4/4 grid — see §2 rhythmic feels) |
| Tonal center | Major, with a minor-6th/diminished vocabulary for color — rarely modal; harmony resolves functionally, not by static vamp |
| Palette (role -> instrument) | Anchor: upright bass or tuba + drums + comping piano/guitar · Front line: trumpet/cornet, clarinet, trombone (Dixieland) or full brass/reed sections (big band) · Ear-candy: mutes, growls, glissandi, stop-time breaks |
| Density ceiling | Dixieland: 3 simultaneous melodic lines (collective improvisation) by design — see §6. Big band: 1 foreground voice/section at a time, everything else is riff or hit. |

These are starting points, not law — bend toward the brief's specific
sub-style while staying inside the classic-jazz family.

## 1. Sub-genres & regional styles

| Sub-genre | Era | Character |
|---|---|---|
| New Orleans / early Dixieland | 1910s-early 1920s | Polyphonic texture, collective improvisation, rooted in marching band, ragtime, and rural blues |
| Chicago-style Dixieland | Late 1920s | Faster, more symmetrical tempos; individual soloists foregrounded; upright bass and acoustic guitar replace tuba/banjo |
| Kansas City swing | Early 1930s | Head arrangements (worked out by ear, riff-based), relaxed and deeply groovy blues feel |
| Harlem / New York big band | 1930s | Duke Ellington / Fletcher Henderson lineage — highly structured, lush melody, block-chord harmony, strong showmanship |
| Sweet swing / commercial big band | Late 1930s-1940s | Radio-oriented (Benny Goodman, Glenn Miller) — precise, clean dynamics, steady tempo built for Lindy Hop |

## 2. Rhythmic feel, grooves & tempo matrix

**Feel types** (name the one the brief wants — they are not interchangeable):

- **2-beat feel** (Dixieland/ragtime) — heavy accent on 1 and 3; bass/tuba
  sounds only on those beats, giving a "bouncing" effect.
- **4-beat straight-ahead swing** — constant accent on 1-2-3-4; bass plays a
  full walking line.
- **Shuffle swing** — the triplet pulse is made very explicit ("da-DUM,
  da-DUM"), a tighter rhythmic push.
- **Charleston rhythm** — the iconic 1920s accent pattern: beat 1 held, beat
  "2-and" hit short.
- **Stop-time** — the whole rhythm section hits together only on beat 1,
  leaving the soloist to fill the rest of the bar unaccompanied.

**Tempo matrix:**

| Category | BPM range | Character & use |
|---|---:|---|
| Slow drag / blues | 60-90 | Dark, sensual, plunger mutes, dragging tempo |
| Ballad swing | 90-110 | Elegant, romantic — ideal for a single horn feature |
| Medium swing | 115-140 | The Lindy Hop sweet spot, very groovy |
| Medium-up swing | 145-180 | Energetic, standard big-band concert tempo |
| Up-tempo / fast | 185-230 | Fast Dixieland, technically showy |
| Virtuoso / burning | 240+ | Wild 1920s party energy, fast Charleston dance |

## 3. Ensemble formats & instrumentation

**A. Dixieland small combo**

- Front line: **Cornet/trumpet** (lead melody), **clarinet** (counter-melody,
  high-register arpeggios and trills), **trombone** (low-register lines,
  glissando "tailgate" style).
- Rhythm section: tenor banjo (4-string), tuba/sousaphone (or upright bass),
  stride piano, vintage drum kit (with woodblock and cowbell).

**B. Swing big band (standard 17-piece)**

- Saxophone/reed section (5): Alto 1 (lead), Alto 2, Tenor 1 (soloist),
  Tenor 2, Baritone. Doubling: clarinet/flute.
- Trumpet section (4): Trumpet 1 (lead, high register), Trumpet 2 (main
  soloist), Trumpet 3 & 4 (harmony).
- Trombone section (4): Trombones 1-3 (tenor trombones), Trombone 4 (bass
  trombone).
- Rhythm section (4): upright bass, acoustic archtop guitar (e.g. Gibson
  L-5), grand piano, vintage drum kit.

**C. Specialist / vintage alternatives**

- Soprano saxophone — a wide-vibrato substitute for clarinet on Dixieland
  melody.
- Bass saxophone / C-melody sax — 1920s-era percussive bassline substitute
  for tuba.
- Jazz violin — fast swiping-bow technique (Gypsy/Hot Club style).
- Vibraphone — adds sparkle above the horn section on ballads/medium swing.
- Washboard — metal-thimble technique, street/novelty-jazz color.

## 4. Brass mute taxonomy

| Mute | Character | Ideal use |
|---|---|---|
| Straight mute | Sharp, nasal, thin, biting | Fast staccato passages, precise accents |
| Cup mute | Muffled, sweet, soft, vintage | 1930s ballads, vocal accompaniment |
| Bucket / velvet mute | Very warm, removes the metallic edge | Introspective solos, dense textures |
| Harmon mute | Buzzing, mysterious, dry | Noir/late-night solo color |
| Plunger mute | "Talking"/wah-wah/growl effect | Traditional blues solos, hot jazz |

## 5. Harmony: vocabulary, voice-leading devices & progressions

**Chord vocabulary:**

- **Major 6th** (C6 = C-E-G-A) — the primary tonic chord, replaces Cmaj7 to
  avoid clashing with the melody's 7th degree.
- **Dominant 7th & 9th** (C7, C9) — the main harmonic driver.
- **Augmented dominant** (C7#5 = C-E-G#-Bb) — floating tension before
  resolving to I.
- **Major 6/9** (C6/9 = C-E-G-A-D) — the classic final-ending chord: lush and
  warm.
- **Minor 6th** (Cm6 = C-Eb-G-A) — sweet minor color for tonic-minor
  function.
- **Diminished 7th** (Cdim7 = C-Eb-Gb-A) — the essential passing/bridge
  chord.
- **Minor-major 7th** (Cm(maj7)) — used exclusively for the minor line
  cliché (below).

**Voice-leading devices:**

- **Major line cliché** — a single voice moves inside a static chord:
  `C -> Cmaj7 -> C7 -> C6` (the note C -> B -> Bb -> A descends over a C
  chord).
- **Minor line cliché** — `Cm -> Cm(maj7) -> Cm7 -> Cm6`.
- **Auxiliary/neighbor diminished** — a diminished chord riding on the same
  root, then resolving back: `C6 -> Cdim7 -> C6`.
- **Backdoor slide dominant** — approach chord I from a half-step above:
  `Db7 -> C6`.

**Voicing techniques:**

- **Freddie Green style (guitar)** — three notes on strings 6, 4, and 3
  (string 5 muted): `[root / 7th / 3rd]` or `[3rd / 7th / 6th]`.
- **Four-way close / block chords (horn section)** — lead melody on top,
  three instruments below it voice the chord tones closely; if the melody
  note is a passing tone outside the chord, harmonize it as a diminished 7th
  chord instead.
- **Drop-2 voicing** — take the second-highest voice of a four-way-close
  chord and drop it an octave, opening the voicing wider and thicker.

**Progression library** (reference by name; pick and adapt, do not restate
verbatim in every song):

```
12-bar classic jazz blues (key of F):
| F6    | Bb7   | F6    | F7    |
| Bb7   | Bbdim7| F6    | D7    |
| Gm7   | C7    | F6 D7 | Gm7 C7|

Rhythm changes - A section (key of Bb):
| Bb  G7  | Cm7 F7  | Bb  G7  | Cm7 F7  |
| Fm7 Bb7 | Eb Edim7| Bb/F F7 | Bb  (F7)|

Rhythm changes - bridge (circle of fifths):
| D7  | D7  | G7  | G7  |
| C7  | C7  | F7  | F7  |

Chromatic-turnaround variant:
| C6 | C#dim7 | Dm7 | D#dim7 |
```

## 6. Melody, motif, ornament, polyphony & call-and-response

**Melodic ornaments:**

- **Enclosure** — surround the target note from above and below before
  landing (landing on E: F -> D# -> E).
- **Chromatic approach** — approach a chord tone a half-step from below
  (landing on G: F# -> G).
- **Arpeggiation + scale descent** — ascend on the chord's arpeggio, descend
  on a blues or major-pentatonic scale.

**Dixieland collective-improvisation roles** (all three lines sound at once
— by design, not an accident of density):

```
Clarinet  : fast arpeggios & high-register ornamentation
Trumpet   : the main melody - syncopated, straight lines
Trombone  : low register - roots, glissando, counter-lines
```

Interaction rule: when the trumpet holds a long note, the clarinet fills the
space with a fast arpeggio, and the trombone fills the bottom with a
glissando/slide line. This is the classic-jazz-specific application of
"who leads/who fills" role assignment — the equivalent of neo-soul's
anchor/rotation/ear-candy model, but simultaneous rather than sequential.

**Call-and-response:**

- **Brass vs. reeds** — brass/trombone "asks" for 2 bars (staccato), the
  saxophones "answer" for 2 bars (legato).
- **Background riffs** — while a soloist plays, the other horn section(s)
  repeat a simple 2-bar motif at low (piano) volume behind them.
- **The solo break** (a load-bearing structural device — do not skip it in
  a big-band arrangement) — the whole band stops dead on beat 1 of bar 4.
  The soloist plays an acrobatic unaccompanied phrase alone for 2 or 4 bars,
  then the full ensemble "explodes" back in together on the next downbeat.

## 7. Comping formulas, groove engine & horn articulation

**Drums:**

- Ride pattern: swung 8ths — `1, 2-and, 3, 4-and` (triplet-based swing).
- Hi-hat: chick, precisely on beats 2 and 4.
- Bass drum: "feathering" — an extremely light tap on every quarter note,
  felt as a pulse rather than heard as a hit.

**Upright bass (walking bass & slap):**

- Walking-bass formula (4/4): root -> 3rd -> 5th -> chromatic approach tone
  into the next chord.
- Slap-bass technique: pluck the string so it snaps back and slaps the
  fretboard (thump-clack), a doubled percussive pulse.

**Piano (stride style):**

- Beats 1 & 3: left hand plays a single low-octave bass note.
- Beats 2 & 4: left hand jumps up to a mid-register chord.

**Guitar (Freddie Green style):**

- Tight 4/4 chunks on every beat, pressed and released quickly for a
  percussive, harmonic pulse (see the voicing in §5).

**Horn articulation & effects** — these carry as much of the classic-jazz
character as the notes themselves:

| Effect | Description |
|---|---|
| Fall-off | The note ends by sliding down quickly |
| Doit | The note ends by sliding dramatically upward |
| Shake | Extreme lip-vibrato on trumpet, a tense/festive color |
| Growl | Singing/shouting into the horn while playing, for a rasping texture |
| Glissando / smear | A continuous pitch slide (a trombone signature) |

Groove detail (tick offsets, gate ratios) lives in
`../../groove-rhythm/references/groove-profiles.md` under the
`classic-jazz-swing` profile — select it by name; do not re-derive
per-note timing here.

## 8. Arrangement blueprints

**Blueprint A — big band swing classic (32-bar AABA form):**

```
[INTRO] -> [HEAD AABA] -> [SOLI SECTION] -> [SOLO BREAK + SOLO] -> [SHOUT CHORUS] -> [OUTRO]
```

- **Intro (8 bar)** — stride piano solo or a drum roll-off, introducing a
  1-6-2-5 vamp.
- **Head / main theme (32-bar AABA):**
  - A1 & A2 (16 bar) — saxophones state the melody; brass answers with
    staccato hits.
  - B/bridge (8 bar) — brief modulation or a clarinet feature with thin
    rhythm-section backing.
  - A3 (8 bar) — the melody returns, played by the full ensemble.
- **Saxophone soli (16 bar)** — the whole reed section (5 players) plays a
  fast, closely harmonized (block-chord) line with precision.
- **Solo section & solo break (32 bar)** — a trumpet solo opens with a 2-bar
  solo break; the other horns play background riffs underneath.
- **Shout chorus (32 bar — the climax)** — the full ensemble plays tutti at
  maximum volume (*ff*), heavily syncopated and dense. Optional: modulate up
  a half-step (e.g. F major -> F# major) here to multiply the energy.
- **Outro (8 bar)** — a big-band tag ending: the closing 2-bar phrase
  repeats 3 times, ending on a held dominant chord (with vibrato) and a
  final staccato hit together on beat 4.

**Blueprint B — Dixieland New Orleans (multi-theme form):**

```
[INTRO] -> [THEME A] -> [THEME B] -> [TRIO (MODULATION)] -> [HOT CHORUS] -> [OUTRO]
```

- **Intro (4 bar)** — banjo and snare play a Charleston pattern.
- **Theme A (16 bar)**, home key — trumpet leads the melody, clarinet
  decorates above it.
- **Theme B (16 bar)** — the melody drops to a lower register, trombone
  becomes more prominent.
- **Trio section (16 bar)** — modulate to the subdominant key; the texture
  softens, clarinet solos.
- **Hot chorus / collective improvisation (16 bar)** — return to the home
  key; all three horns improvise together freely (§6), drums play a
  press-roll.
- **Outro (4 bar)** — Dixieland tag ending: `I6 - bIII7 - II7 - bII7 | I6`
  (e.g. in C: `C6 - Eb7 - D7 - Db7 | C6`).

## 9. Vintage production intent (downstream — not this package's job)

This package composes and arranges; it does not mix audio (see
`README.md` — this is Tool 1, "the brain"; rendering to a finished mix is a
separate downstream engine/DAW step). The production reference brief
included mix intent for a 1920s/1930s analog character — record it here as
**intent to hand to whoever mixes the render**, not as something this
package's scripts implement:

```
[ natural acoustic ] -> [ ribbon mic emulation ] -> [ tape saturation ] -> [ tube EQ roll-off ]
```

- High-cut above 10-12 kHz; low-cut below 45-50 Hz (no electronic sub-bass
  in this era).
- Wide-Q midrange boost, +2 dB around 1.5-3 kHz, for a gramophone-era
  character.
- Ribbon mic emulation (e.g. RCA 44-BX modeling) on horns to soften upper
  transients; tape saturation on the master bus; slow optical/VCA bus
  compression, 1-3 dB gain reduction.
- Short room/chamber reverb (1.2-1.6s decay) — avoid digital plate or large
  hall algorithms.

## Common Pitfalls

| Symptom | Fix |
|---|---|
| Dixieland lines all play the same rhythm/register | Enforce the three distinct roles in §6 — clarinet high/fast, trumpet mid/melodic, trombone low/glissando |
| Big-band arrangement never gives a soloist an unaccompanied moment | Add the solo break (§6) — it is structural, not optional |
| Harmony sounds "modern jazz" (dense altered extensions everywhere) | Lean on §5's vocabulary — 6th chords, diminished passing chords, augmented dominants; save altered/upper-structure harmony for other genres |
| Shout chorus arrives with no build | Confirm the form actually passes through soli -> solo break -> solo before the shout chorus (Blueprint A) |
| Groove feels stiff/quantized | Select the `classic-jazz-swing` groove profile (§7) rather than a straight or lofi pocket |
| Horns played with no articulation variety | Assign fall-offs/doits/shakes/growls/glissandi (§7) deliberately per phrase, not uniformly |
| Ensemble sounds thin for its instrumentation | Confirm the ensemble format (§3) matches the arrangement blueprint — a Dixieland combo cannot fill a shout chorus written for 17 pieces |
| Mixing the render like a modern track | Treat §9 as intent for the downstream mix step, not a request to this package |
