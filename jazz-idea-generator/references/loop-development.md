## 1. Loop as seed, not finished form

A loop is a repeatable identity unit. A composition requires expectation, development, contrast, return, and ending. Do not confuse adding layers with composing a form.

Use this reference for lofi, jazzhop, neo-soul, smooth jazz, electronic jazz, fusion vamps, groove pieces, and any brief built from four-, eight-, or sixteen-bar material.

## 2. Define loop DNA

Record the following before extending the loop:

- length in bars and beats;
- harmonic rhythm;
- bass contour;
- top-note or guide-tone line;
- motif rhythm and interval shape;
- drum reference layer;
- characteristic silence;
- voicing register and attack profile;
- loop seam: what connects the last event back to the first;
- closure type: closed, circular, suspended, or deceptive.

Choose two or three protected features. Mutations must retain at least one protected feature.

## 3. Mutation classes

### Harmonic mutations

- inversion change;
- bass substitution;
- one-chord reharmonization;
- tonicization of a phrase-end target;
- pedal under changing upper structures;
- common-tone substitute;
- delayed resolution;
- shortened or extended final chord;
- harmonic dropout while melody or bass continues.

### Melodic mutations

- change only the final note;
- fragment the first half;
- answer in another register;
- alter onset but preserve contour;
- preserve rhythm with new chord-degree mapping;
- introduce a countermelody;
- turn the hook into a background figure;
- leave a deliberate rest where the hook was expected.

### Bass mutations

- root to inversion;
- held pedal to approach line;
- octave displacement;
- anticipatory pickup;
- rhythmic simplification;
- chromatic walk into a target;
- temporary silence;
- contrary motion against the top line.

### Drum mutations

- remove kick events;
- change hi-hat subdivision;
- open or close the hat at a phrase boundary;
- add ghost-note response;
- half-time or double-time implication;
- one-beat fill;
- stop-time hit;
- percussion entry or exit;
- preserve backbeat while changing everything else.

### Voicing mutations

- move one inner voice by semitone;
- change spread without changing pitch classes;
- move the melody note between top and inner voice;
- replace rootless voicing with shell or dyad;
- change attack order;
- shorten note-off time;
- omit one color tone;
- transfer a voicing to another instrument.

### Texture and production mutations

- filter state;
- room size or send change;
- mono/stereo width change;
- resampled or degraded version;
- reverse pickup from original material;
- foreground/background exchange;
- high- or low-register layer removal;
- momentary dry signal;
- silence or tail-only transition.

## 4. Development timescales

### Every one to four bars

Allow subtle human or phrase variation. Do not force a conspicuous event every bar.

### Every four to eight bars

Introduce a phrase marker:

- fill;
- pickup;
- altered cadence;
- motif answer;
- one layer entry or exit;
- changed chord duration;
- brief silence.

### Every eight to sixteen bars

Change section state or listener interpretation:

- full hook versus fragment;
- bass state change;
- new counterline;
- new voicing family;
- reduced drums;
- harmonic side-step;
- instrumentation handoff.

### Every sixteen to thirty-two bars

Create a formal event:

- new section;
- breakdown;
- solo or open development;
- contrasting field;
- transformed return;
- global climax;
- outro process.

These intervals are planning ranges, not mandatory clockwork. Align changes with phrase logic.

## 5. Loop-to-song workflow

1. write and validate the core loop;
2. create a reduced version containing only essential DNA;
3. create an expanded version with one new role, not every possible layer;
4. create a contrasting section that preserves one invariant;
5. design the transition into the contrast;
6. create an open, solo, or developmental section;
7. plan a return that reveals changed meaning;
8. design a finite outro or live ending;
9. assign every recurrence a variation status: literal, micro-varied, reorchestrated, reharmonized, fragmented, or transformed;
10. remove events that do not change perception.

## 6. Instrumental section functions

Instrumental music still needs functional sections. Useful roles include:

- identity reveal;
- groove establishment;
- theme statement;
- expansion;
- response or counterstatement;
- harmonic contrast;
- solo or open development;
- breakdown;
- rebuild;
- transformed theme return;
- release;
- coda or decay.

Do not label sections only `A`, `B`, and `C` in the design dossier. State what each section does.

## 7. Recurrence strategy

For every repeated section, choose one status:

- **literal:** reserved for establishing identity or deliberate trance;
- **micro-varied:** same structure, altered local detail;
- **reorchestrated:** same notes, changed roles or register;
- **reharmonized:** same motif, changed harmonic reading;
- **rhythmically reframed:** same pitches, changed subdivision or pocket;
- **fragmented:** only a recognizable part returns;
- **expanded:** original material gains a response or extension;
- **compressed:** phrase is shortened to accelerate form;
- **transformed:** identity remains, but multiple parameters change.

Avoid using literal recurrence more than twice in a short instrumental unless repetition itself is the concept.

## 8. Energy and density curves

Track at least four independent curves from 1 to 5:

- rhythmic activity;
- harmonic tension;
- register height;
- texture density.

Optional curves:

- stereo width;
- improvisational freedom;
- timbral brightness;
- microtiming looseness.

Do not make every curve rise together. A convincing climax may have high register and harmonic tension but low density. A breakdown may retain rhythmic energy while removing harmony.

## 9. Example 64-bar architecture

| Bars | Function | Identity state | Main change | Transition |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| 1-4 | cold identity preview | hook fragment | filtered harmony only | tail into groove |
| 5-12 | groove establishment | core loop | bass and reduced drums | pickup fill |
| 13-20 | full theme | complete hook | full pocket and exact voicing path | one-bar subtraction |
| 21-28 | theme development | micro-varied | counterline and bass inversion | harmonic connector |
| 29-36 | contrast | motif rhythm retained | new harmonic field, reduced drums | reverse pickup or live swell |
| 37-44 | solo/open development | hook absent but implied | interaction and register growth | bass cue |
| 45-52 | transformed return | hook restored | reharmonized phrase end, fuller texture | stop-time break |
| 53-60 | final statement | compressed hook | highest register or strongest groove | two-bar tag setup |
| 61-64 | outro | hook fragment | layer subtraction and finite cadence | final cutoff |

Adapt length to tempo and target duration. Do not copy this architecture mechanically.

## 10. Loop audit

- Is the loop seam intentional?
- Can the DNA be named in one sentence?
- Does each recurrence have a declared status?
- Is there at least one meaningful subtraction event?
- Does the contrasting section preserve an invariant?
- Are changes coordinated across layers?
- Is the solo or open section connected to the composition?
- Does the return contain new information?
- Is the outro performable without relying only on fade?
- Does the total form fit the requested duration?
