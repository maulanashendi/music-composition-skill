# Form, phrase architecture, and dramaturgy

> Adapted from `jazz-composition-designer` (archived portable skill package,
> consolidated 2026-07-13). Read this alongside `ideation-theory.md` §4b–4c
> and §7: that file gives the *dramatic arc* (the emotional journey that
> decides everything) and a compact structure vocabulary; this file goes
> deeper on the mechanics of form itself — bar counts, phrase length,
> section-contrast dimensions, cadence strength, solo form, and intro/ending
> strategy. Use the arc from `ideation-theory.md` to choose *which* formal
> family and phrase design serve the piece, not the reverse.

## 1. Form is recurrence plus contrast

Letters such as AABA describe related or contrasting material, not genre. Define exact bar counts, repeats, first/second endings, and transitions.

## 2. Common formal families

### Blues

- 12 bars is common, but 8-, 16-, and expanded blues forms exist.
- Structural landmarks often occur at bars 1, 5, 9, and the turnaround.

### 32-bar song forms

- AABA: statement, restatement, bridge, return.
- ABAC: repeated opening with two different continuations.
- AABC, ABAB, and variants are valid.

Do not force every standard-like composition into 32 bars.

### 16- and 24-bar forms

Useful for concise themes, modal pieces, and modern standards. Maintain phrase clarity.

### Verse-chorus and song forms

Appropriate for vocal, smooth, contemporary, jazz-pop, and hybrid compositions. Distinguish pop `chorus` from jazz `one chorus of the form`. Assign labels by audible function rather than position — hook-first openings, verses, pre-choruses, refrains, post-choruses, bridges, solo integration, tags, codas, and outros each do a distinct job.

### Vamp/riff forms

Organize through texture, layering, solo entries, and cue-based transitions rather than many chord changes.

### Through-composed and episodic

Use for chamber jazz, third stream, fusion suites, narrative works, and complex meters. Provide recognizable anchors or processes.

### Open/cue-based forms

Define event order, cue authority, duration ranges, and return points.

## 3. Phrase length

Use regular or irregular phrases intentionally:

- 4+4;
- 2+2+4;
- 3+5;
- 5+5+6;
- elision where one phrase ending begins the next;
- extension or truncation at climactic points.

Irregularity should be audible as design, not accidental bar-count error.

## 4. Section contrast

Create contrast through selected dimensions:

- tonal center or mode;
- harmonic rhythm;
- bass behavior;
- meter or subdivision;
- motif register/contour;
- orchestration;
- density and dynamics;
- articulation;
- fixed versus open material.

Retain one invariant to preserve identity.

## 5. Formal harmonic strategy

Assign phrase-end strength:

- weak continuation;
- half cadence/suspension;
- local arrival;
- global cadence;
- deceptive extension;
- open-ended cue.

Do not give every four bars the same cadence.

## 6. Solo form

Choose among:

- same form as head;
- simplified changes;
- separate vamp;
- alternate open section;
- backgrounds entering on later choruses;
- form shortened/expanded by cue.

State how players track the form and re-enter the head.

## 7. Intro strategies

- composed thematic fragment;
- vamp;
- last four/eight bars of form;
- rubato cadenza;
- drum/bass groove reveal;
- free texture resolving into countable time;
- chorale or counterpoint.

The intro should establish or strategically withhold key, tempo, motif, or groove.

## 8. Ending strategies

- final cadence and held chord;
- tag of last two/four bars;
- vamp with cue;
- stop ending;
- ritardando/rubato coda;
- reharmonized final phrase;
- fade concept for production, with live alternative;
- open cutoff cue.

Write exact cue and bar count.

## 9. Dramatic arc

The arc itself — the emotional journey and the seven dimensions it moves (dynamics, register, density, harmonic tension, rhythmic subdivision, orchestration, improvisational freedom) — is defined once in `ideation-theory.md` §4b–4c; use that as the master. The only form-specific note here: a typical arc may build, drop, and rebuild, but other shapes are valid, and the final head must not feel mechanically pasted after solos.

## 10. Duration planning

Calculate approximate performance length from:

- BPM and beats per bar;
- bars per form;
- head in/out;
- solo choruses;
- open sections;
- intros/interludes/coda;
- expected rubato.

State whether the estimate assumes no repeats or performance repeats.

## 11. Form audit

- Do bar counts add up?
- Are repeats/endings unambiguous?
- Does each labeled song section perform the function implied by its name?
- Is the hook distinct from the chorus or refrain when the design requires that distinction?
- Does the bridge or contrasting section earn its role?
- Is the solo architecture compatible with the head?
- Are transitions cued?
- Does the performance arc have a clear beginning, development, and ending?
